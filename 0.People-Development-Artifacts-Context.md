# Context: People Development Artifacts and Talent Development Architecture

## Purpose

This context packet summarizes the reusable management architecture found in the old people-development artifacts under `context_artifacts/` and explains how that material can support `Talent-Development-Architecture.md` and the remaining gaps in `0.Organizational-Excellence-Book-Gaps.md`.

The raw artifacts should be treated as private source material, not publishable content. They contain former-employer names, confidential labels, personal names, manager names, internal project names, old platform/vendor references, metadata, and personal review material. The useful material is not the wording, branding, or templates themselves. The useful material is the operating pattern underneath them.

That pattern is this:

> A serious talent system turns career aspiration into role context, role context into competency targets, competency targets into observed evidence, observed evidence into development plans, development plans into real assignments, and real assignments back into reviewable evidence.

That is almost exactly the argument Talent Development Architecture is already making. The old artifacts are not a model to copy. They are evidence that the pieces of a closed-loop development system already exist in ordinary enterprise practice, even when the enterprise fails to connect them into one coherent architecture.

## Sanitization Boundary

Do not quote the raw artifacts in reader-facing documents.

Do not publish screenshots, slide layouts, extracted text, company-specific competency libraries, internal role maps, employee names, manager names, project names, business-unit names, vendor names, proprietary metrics, or old review language.

Use the artifacts only at the level of sanitized mechanism:

- career map
- role family
- current role
- target role
- competency target
- proficiency level
- self assessment
- manager calibration
- one-on-one discussion
- development plan
- stretch assignment
- mentor or coach input
- formal training
- quarterly check-in
- performance evidence
- promotion or mobility discussion

The source directory is already ignored by `.gitignore`. Keep it that way. The public repo should contain only generalized synthesis, not employer source material.

## Source Material Reviewed

The artifact set falls into seven sanitized categories:

1. **Career development guide**
   - A large internal guide with role families, generic jobs, career paths, job types, move types, competency groups, and proficiency levels.
   - Reusable mechanism: a career system needs a map of possible roles, the competencies attached to those roles, and a way to distinguish lateral growth, expanded scope, and upward movement.

2. **Competency assessment tool**
   - A structured self-assessment and manager-calibration artifact.
   - Reusable mechanism: competency gaps should be compared against a target role or current-role expectation, then discussed with a manager rather than treated as private self-reflection.

3. **70/20/10 development plan**
   - A planning artifact that separates development into on-the-job experience, learning from others, and formal training.
   - Reusable mechanism: development plans should privilege real work and feedback over classroom completion, while still leaving room for formal study.

4. **Behavioral interview question bank**
   - A structured set of interview prompts grouped by values, behaviors, and work situations.
   - Reusable mechanism: hiring should gather comparable behavioral evidence rather than letting each interviewer invent a different signal model.

5. **Personal performance and development review**
   - A review artifact connecting accountabilities, measures, mid-year feedback, year-end feedback, development goals, operational projects, knowledge sharing, process improvement, and manager comments.
   - Reusable mechanism: performance evidence is strongest when it ties work, outcomes, development, and manager observation into one record.

6. **Staff development / PDP slide templates**
   - Career discussion templates built around current role, level, time in level, education, certifications, accomplishments, strengths, improvement areas, short-term development, longer-term development, assignments, systems to learn, and training to complete.
   - Reusable mechanism: career conversations need a portable packet that lets a panel or manager understand the person in context, not merely by title or tenure.

7. **Interview / internal mobility presentation**
   - A candidate-style presentation with career background, technical competencies, training, accomplishments, development needs, and plans for the team.
   - Reusable mechanism: internal mobility and promotion decisions benefit from structured evidence packets, but these can easily drift into narrative performance theater unless the evidence is tied to real decision criteria.

## Core Thesis

The artifacts support a sharper version of the TDA thesis:

> Talent development fails when the organization has fragments of a development system but no loop connecting them.

A career guide without real assignments becomes career-pathing theater.

A competency rubric without observation becomes vocabulary theater.

A development plan without work allocation becomes self-improvement theater.

A behavioral interview guide without calibration becomes question-bank theater.

A performance review without preserved context becomes memory theater.

Talent Development Architecture should name this clearly. The failure is often not that the organization has no tools. The failure is that each tool lives in its own HR, manager, interview, learning, or review container. The employee is expected to integrate the system alone.

The TDA move is to make the organization own the integration.

## How This Supports Talent Development Architecture

### 1. Career Pathing Needs a Real Map

The career-guide material strongly supports the TDA section on career-pathing theater.

The old guide distinguishes among role families, generic jobs, job types, lateral moves, expanded-scope moves, discovery assignments, and upward movement. That is valuable because it prevents "career path" from meaning only promotion.

The useful sanitized distinction:

- **Move upward:** increased scope, responsibility, complexity, or decision rights.
- **Move sideways:** broader experience at a similar level.
- **Build in place:** new responsibility inside the current role.
- **Discover:** short-term exposure used to test interest and fit.

TDA should use this structure to clarify that development architecture needs more than a ladder. A ladder only explains vertical progression. People also need lateral moves, short-term discovery assignments, internal shadowing, deeper current-role scope, and bounded access to adjacent work.

The caution is just as important: generic role maps do not automatically create opportunity. They become useful only when connected to open work, business need, manager coordination, and evidence of current and future proficiency.

### 2. Competencies Can Define Transition Gates

The competency material supports TDA's "career transition gate" and "safe-reps path" concepts.

A useful competency model does not merely say "this person is good at X." It describes what a higher operating mode looks like. The old artifacts use multiple proficiency levels, but the public argument should avoid copying those levels. The reusable idea is that each role-relevant capability should have observable stages:

- awareness
- routine execution
- independent execution
- broader coordination
- system-level ownership

Those stages can become transition gates.

For TDA, this matters because the hardest development jump is often not a tool or skill jump. It is an operating-mode jump. The employee moves from doing the work, to improving the work, to coordinating the work, to changing the system around the work.

Competency language can help only if it names observable behavior. If it slides into personality descriptors, it weakens the whole system. Keep TDA's existing discipline: behavior, not personality; evidence, not impressions.

### 3. 70/20/10 Is a Safe-Reps Planning Shape

The development-plan material maps cleanly onto TDA's safe-reps argument.

The reusable 70/20/10 mechanism is not the exact ratio. The ratio is a useful reminder, but the deeper point is that most development happens through real work, feedback from other people, and deliberate practice. Formal training matters, but it is rarely enough by itself.

For TDA, this suggests a clean development-plan shape:

| Development Need | Real Work Rep | Feedback Source | Formal Support | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What the person is trying to build | The bounded assignment where the skill will be practiced | Manager, mentor, peer, work owner, or community | Course, reading, lab, certification, or reference material | What will show that the person has actually improved |

This is stronger than a normal training plan because it keeps learning attached to work. It also prevents the common failure where an employee completes a course and the organization quietly treats that as capability.

Training is not evidence of readiness. It is input to readiness. The evidence comes from observed behavior under realistic conditions.

### 4. Manager Calibration Makes Self-Assessment Useful

The competency assessment artifact has a useful sequence:

1. The employee identifies relevant competencies.
2. The employee performs a self assessment.
3. The manager reviews the same target.
4. The manager provides context.
5. The employee and manager discuss the gap.
6. The gap becomes a development plan.

This supports TDA's claim that one-on-ones are management infrastructure. Self-reflection matters, but it is not enough. Manager judgment matters, but it is dangerous if it arrives as unilateral verdict. The point is the comparison.

The employee's self assessment reveals their mental model. The manager's assessment reveals the organization's expectation. The development conversation should inspect the difference without turning it into a personality trial.

This also gives TDA a concrete way to connect coaching and competency systems:

> A competency gap is not the coaching conversation. It is the opening evidence for one.

The manager still has to ask what the person thought success required, what signals they saw, what signals they missed, what support existed, and what safe reps can close the gap.

### 5. Behavioral Interviewing Belongs in the Evidence System

The behavioral interview artifact supports the planned Hiring Guide.

Its useful mechanism is simple: interviewers should ask candidates for concrete situations, choices, tradeoffs, actions, and results. That gives the organization a better chance of comparing evidence across candidates.

The book should not reuse the old question bank. It should reuse the design rule:

> A hiring process should decide which behaviors matter before the interview, then ask questions that expose comparable evidence for those behaviors.

This pairs directly with TDA:

- Hiring is external talent intake.
- TDA is internal talent development.
- Both need evidence models.
- Both fail when the organization relies on vibes, charisma, pedigree, urgency, or one interviewer's favorite proxy.

The planned Hiring Guide should connect behavioral interviewing to role evidence, competency targets, work samples, reference context, and post-hire development planning. If hiring discovers a candidate's likely development needs, those needs should not disappear after the offer. They should become early safe-reps planning and manager support.

### 6. Staff Development Panels Are an Internal Mobility Primitive

The staff-development slide templates suggest a useful internal mobility mechanism.

The template asks for current role, current level, time in level, accomplishments, strengths, improvement areas, desired role type, short-term development, longer-term development, assignments or projects, systems to learn, and training to complete.

Sanitized, that becomes a strong "internal talent packet":

- current role and scope
- demonstrated accomplishments
- current operating level
- desired next operating mode
- strengths to preserve
- gaps to close
- experiences needed
- systems or domains to learn
- proposed assignments
- manager or panel assessment

This supports TDA's Internal Farm System. If internal mobility depends only on informal networks, employees with better social access get better development. A portable internal talent packet gives managers and receiving teams a cleaner way to discuss readiness, fit, supervision, and risk.

The caution: the packet must serve assignment design, not self-marketing. If the packet becomes a beauty contest, the organization has only created another layer of performance theater.

### 7. Performance Plans Can Become Evidence Ledgers

The personal review artifact is useful because it ties accountabilities, measures, operational work, development actions, self reflection, and manager feedback into one timeline.

The public work should not reuse the details. The pattern matters:

- What was the person accountable for?
- What measures or observations were expected?
- What work actually happened?
- What changed mid-year?
- What evidence did the employee provide?
- What evidence did the manager confirm?
- What development work happened?
- What new capability became visible?
- What should happen next?

This belongs in TDA's evidence loop. A review should not be an annual act of memory reconstruction. It should be a reconciliation point for evidence gathered during the year.

This also connects to the book's larger closed-loop thesis. A performance plan is a forecast. It says what the organization expects the person to deliver, learn, and demonstrate. A review should reconcile that forecast against reality, then feed the learning into the next plan.

## Gaps This Material Helps Fill

### One-on-Ones as Management Infrastructure

The artifact set gives enough source material to justify a dedicated chapter.

Working thesis:

> One-on-ones are not status meetings. They are the management control surface for utilization, development, coaching, preference signal, constraint discovery, and evidence capture.

Possible chapter spine:

1. What one-on-ones are for.
2. What they should not become.
3. The minimum signal a manager should maintain.
4. Career alignment questions.
5. Utilization review questions.
6. Safe-reps and stretch-proposal review.
7. Coaching follow-up.
8. Evidence capture.
9. When the conversation becomes a formal performance artifact.
10. What should and should not roll upward.

This chapter would let TDA stay conceptual while giving managers the recurring operating ritual.

### Hiring Guide

The behavioral interview artifact gives the Hiring Guide a concrete starting point, but the book should go beyond interview prompts.

Working thesis:

> Hiring is external capability acquisition, and the hiring process should preserve the evidence needed to understand what capability was actually acquired, what assumptions were made, and what support the person will need after joining.

Useful sections:

- role evidence before requisition
- competency and operating-mode targets
- resume/CV context as metadata, not trivia
- behavioral evidence
- work-sample evidence
- interviewer calibration
- decision record
- post-hire development handoff

This pairs with TDA because hiring and internal development are two answers to the same capability question.

### Facts vs Stories in People Systems

TDA already has a note that facts vs stories deserves more than a single sentence. The artifacts reinforce that need.

People systems are full of story-shaped records: interview answers, manager comments, self reviews, development goals, feedback, and panel impressions. Those stories are not useless. They contain context. But they become dangerous when the organization forgets which parts are facts, which parts are interpretation, and which parts are untested claims.

Possible new gap:

**Facts, Stories, and Evidence in People Decisions**

Working thesis:

> People decisions require narrative context, but narrative context must be disciplined by evidence labels. The organization should know whether it is looking at an observed behavior, a self report, a manager interpretation, a secondhand claim, an outcome measure, or an assumption.

This belongs near coaching, calibration, hiring, and performance review. It also connects to the broader book thesis because the same problem appears in RFPs, risk records, post-implementation reviews, and executive reporting.

### Operating Cadence

The artifacts imply a cadence even when they do not name it as architecture:

- development conversations around review cycles
- quarterly or periodic development-plan checks
- manager calibration after self assessment
- career-panel or mobility discussion
- mid-year and year-end reconciliation
- training follow-up
- evidence from actual work

This supports the missing Operating Cadence chapter. The people-system cadence should be one part of the enterprise cadence, not an HR side quest.

Possible cadence:

- monthly one-on-one for signal maintenance
- quarterly development-plan check
- semiannual competency or career alignment review
- annual performance reconciliation
- event-driven review after major stretch assignments, role changes, incidents, promotions, or performance regressions

The key is that each cadence produces or updates an artifact. A meeting that changes nothing in the evidence system is usually a conversation, not a loop.

### Benefits Realization and Post-Implementation Review

This material also supports the benefits-realization gap, although indirectly.

A development plan is a small benefits-realization system. It states what capability the person intends to build, what actions they will take, what support they need, and what evidence should show progress. Later, the person and manager compare the plan against what happened.

That same shape applies to project funding, vendor selection, architecture decisions, and process improvement. The people-system artifacts can help the book show that closed-loop thinking is not only a project-management pattern. It is a general operating discipline.

### Executive Translation Layer

The artifacts also demonstrate what should not roll upward.

Executives need aggregate signal:

- capability gaps
- succession risk
- development bottlenecks
- role families with weak internal pipeline
- skills the organization keeps buying externally
- areas where safe-reps paths do not exist
- evidence that development investment is or is not producing capability

They should not receive raw one-on-one notes, private coaching details, uncontextualized self assessments, or performance narratives stripped of evidence labels.

This gives the Executive Translation Layer a people-system case study: preserve signal without turning developmental records into surveillance.

## Reusable Closed-Loop Model

The sanitized model that can be reused across TDA and future chapters:

1. **Role Context**
   - What is the current role?
   - What role, scope, domain, or operating mode might come next?
   - Is the target real, available, and useful to the organization?

2. **Competency Target**
   - What capability does the target require?
   - What does better performance look like in observable terms?
   - What transition gate is the person trying to cross?

3. **Evidence Baseline**
   - What has the person already demonstrated?
   - What does the employee believe they can do?
   - What has the manager observed?
   - Where do those reads differ?

4. **Development Plan**
   - What real work will provide the rep?
   - Who will provide feedback?
   - What formal learning or reference material supports the rep?
   - What risks need boundaries?

5. **Safe-Reps Assignment**
   - What can the person do independently?
   - What requires approval?
   - What must remain read-only, shadowed, or supervised?
   - What would reasonable struggle look like?
   - What would unacceptable risk look like?

6. **Checkpoint**
   - What changed?
   - What evidence was produced?
   - What support was missing?
   - What should be adjusted?

7. **Reconciliation**
   - Did the person build the intended capability?
   - Was the original target right?
   - Did the organization provide the promised support?
   - Is the person ready for more scope, a different assignment, more practice, or a different path?

8. **Next Decision**
   - Continue current plan.
   - Adjust assignment.
   - Add support.
   - Create a new safe-reps path.
   - Move the person.
   - Promote the person.
   - Hire externally.
   - Change the system because the blocker is not the person.

## Possible Additions to TDA

### Add Career Movement Types

In `Career Pathing Needs Architecture`, add a short distinction among upward moves, lateral moves, current-role expansion, and discovery assignments. That would give the career architecture more shape than promotion alone.

### Add Competency Gaps as Transition Gates

In `Every Career Transition Gate Needs a Safe-Reps Path`, add a paragraph explaining that competency rubrics are useful only when they become transition gates with observable evidence and bounded practice paths.

### Add 70/20/10 as a Sanitized Development-Plan Pattern

In `Stretch Proposals` or `Safe Reps`, add a small planning table that separates real-work reps, feedback sources, formal learning, and evidence.

### Add Manager Calibration to One-on-Ones

In `One-on-Ones`, add language that self assessment and manager assessment should meet in the one-on-one, because the difference between those views is often where the real coaching signal lives.

### Add Evidence Labels

Near the existing facts-vs-stories note, add a stronger concept:

> People records should label evidence. An observed behavior, self report, manager interpretation, secondhand claim, outcome measure, and assumption should not be treated as the same kind of fact.

## The Important Caution

The old artifacts prove a useful point: enterprises often have the ingredients of a development system and still fail to develop people well.

The missing piece is integration.

If a career guide lives on one site, a competency model lives in another template, development goals live in a review system, stretch work happens through side channels, coaching lives in private manager memory, and promotion decisions happen in rooms the employee cannot see, then the organization has artifacts without architecture.

Talent Development Architecture should not argue for more forms. It should argue for connected loops.

The goal is not to make talent development more bureaucratic. The goal is to make it harder for the organization to throw away the evidence it already worked to create.
