# Organizational Excellence - Book Architecture and Gap Map

*Working brief for turning the current organizational-philosophy document library into a coherent book-length guide. This file is intentionally separate from `0.Organizational-Philosophy-Docs-Context-and-Roadmap.md`: that document tracks the original doc family and design invariants; this one tracks the broader book architecture, missing chapters, and how the current docs become a complete system.*

---

## Working Book Thesis

**Enterprises suck because they run open-loop: they make requests, predictions, judgments, vendor selections, hiring decisions, priority calls, architecture choices, and performance assessments, then fail to preserve the evidence needed to reconcile those decisions against reality.**

The book's job is to show how to make an enterprise suck less by closing those loops:

1. Record the judgment or prediction when it is made.
2. Preserve the context, assumptions, constraints, and expected outcome.
3. Route the artifact somewhere it must be read.
4. Reconcile the original claim against what actually happened.
5. Feed the learning back into the next decision.

The current document set already covers several components of this system. The gap is the map: readers need to see how work definition, architecture framing, process improvement, career systems, coaching, RFPs, risk capture, AI leverage, and executive reporting all fit into one enterprise operating model.

## Proposed Book Structure

### Part 1 - The Open-Loop Enterprise

Purpose: explain the core failure pattern.

Planned chapters:

- **The Enterprise Throws Away the Answer Key**
  - Open loops in people, projects, vendors, architecture, risk, process, and hiring.
  - Written records are not bureaucracy by default; they are organizational memory.
  - The issue is not that judgment is bad. The issue is that unrecorded judgment cannot be reconciled.

- **The Closed-Loop Rule**
  - Every important decision needs a durable claim, owner, expected outcome, evidence path, and review point.
  - The same pattern recurs whether the subject is a promotion packet, an RFP, a project business case, a risk find, or an architecture decision.

Gap: this part does not exist yet as a reader-facing chapter. The current roadmap has the thesis, but it is written as internal context rather than as the opening argument of a book.

### Part 2 - Designing Work Before Doing Work

Purpose: show how to prevent bad execution by improving the quality of the request, classification, and design frame.

Current chapters:

- `personal-decision-framework.md`
- `Writing Work Items - Epics, Stories, and Tasks.md`
- `framing-questions-for-platform-architecture-and-design.md`

Missing chapters:

- **Enterprise Intake and Prioritization**
  - How work enters the organization.
  - Who is allowed to ask for work.
  - What information must exist before prioritization.
  - How priority decisions are recorded.
  - How demand gets converted into roadmap work, rejected, deferred, or turned into standard work.

- **Decision Records and Governance**
  - What kinds of decisions require a record.
  - How reversibility changes the process.
  - Who owns the decision and who owns later reconciliation.
  - When decisions must be revisited.
  - How to avoid ADR theater: a decision record that nobody reads is not a loop.

- **RFPs and Vendor Selection as Evidence Systems**
  - Captured below as a planned chapter skeleton.

### Part 3 - Operating the System

Purpose: show how the organization runs and improves once work is visible.

Current chapter:

- `Process-Improvement-Framework.md`

Missing or partial chapters:

- **Operating Cadence**
  - Weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annual loops.
  - Which meetings exist, which artifacts they produce, and which decisions they are allowed to make.
  - One-on-ones, retrospectives, intake review, risk review, calibration, portfolio review, and post-implementation review as separate loops.

- **Risk Find / Significant-Issue Capture**
  - Already planned in `0.Organizational-Philosophy-Docs-Context-and-Roadmap.md`.
  - Should become the standard for recording high-value discoveries that do not fit normal bug/ticket systems.

- **Implementation Roadmap**
  - `Process-Improvement-Framework.md` already begins this, especially with visibility, maturity, work taxonomy, standardization, automation, and metrics.
  - Needs expansion into a broader adoption sequence for the whole book:
    1. Make work visible.
    2. Standardize intake and work types.
    3. Define completion conditions.
    4. Record decisions and assumptions.
    5. Add risk-find capture.
    6. Add post-implementation review.
    7. Add people-system evidence loops.
    8. Add leverage metrics only after enough data exists.

- `DevOps-SRE-Coaching-and-Calibration-Guide.md`

  - Mentioned in TDA.

### Part 5 - Closing the Loop at Enterprise Scale

Purpose: show how the artifacts roll up without destroying the signal.

Missing chapters:

- **Benefits Realization and Post-Implementation Review**
  - Compare business cases, expected benefits, and actual outcomes.
  - Convert project funding from one-time persuasion into a forecast that later gets graded.

- **Engineering Leverage Metrics**
  - Already planned.
  - Should wait until capture layers exist.
  - Record the plays before computing the leverage.

- **AI and Organizational Leverage**
  - Needs its own chapter or chapter cluster.
  - Source material to incorporate later:
    - AI talk-prep notes and visuals maintained outside this repository.
  - Likely themes:
    - AI collapses the activation energy of high-value, deferred-payoff documentation work.
    - AI is best aimed at economically stranded toil.
    - AI should generate discussion artifacts, not replace judgment.
    - AI makes loop theater cheaper too, so routing and reconciliation matter more.

- **Executive Translation Layer**
  - What leaders should ask for.
  - What artifacts should travel upward.
  - What artifacts must not travel upward, especially retrospectives.
  - What reports are useful without turning learning loops into performance theater.

## RFP / Vendor Selection Chapter Skeleton

Working title: **RFPs and Vendor Selection as Evidence Systems**

Working thesis: **An RFP is not a purchasing ritual. It is a structured method for recording organizational need, vendor claims, evaluation criteria, assumptions, tradeoffs, and risk acceptance before money changes hands.**

Source note: the RFP model is strongly informed by federal-style acquisition practice, especially the strict process discipline used in high-risk infrastructure procurement. No research is needed before drafting the first version; the task is to organize the already-known framework into the broader closed-loop model.

### 1. Why RFPs Fail

Failure modes to cover:

- The organization cannot state what it actually needs.
- Requirements are written as solution preferences instead of capability needs.
- Evaluation criteria are invented after preferences have already formed.
- Demos are mistaken for evidence.
- Procurement, operators, security, engineering, finance, and support evaluate different realities.
- Vendor promises are not preserved in a form that can be graded later.
- The selected tool creates operational burden that was not included in the decision.

### 2. Before the RFP

The RFP process should start before the RFP document exists.

Required pre-work:

- Problem statement.
- Current-state pain.
- Target capability.
- Non-goals.
- Constraints.
- Operating model.
- Integration assumptions.
- Security/compliance constraints.
- Success measures.
- Support model.
- Reversibility or exit strategy.
- Budget and timeline realities.

Closed loop: this records what the organization believed it needed before vendors had a chance to reshape the question.

### 3. Requirements Structure

Separate requirements by kind:

- Functional requirements.
- Non-functional requirements.
- Operational requirements.
- Security requirements.
- Integration requirements.
- Reporting/audit requirements.
- Support and lifecycle requirements.
- Migration requirements.
- Exit requirements.

Important distinction: a requirement should say what must be true, not which vendor-specific mechanism must be used, unless the mechanism is genuinely non-negotiable.

### 4. The Evaluation Model

The evaluation model must exist before responses are scored.

Possible components:

- Must-have gates.
- Disqualifiers.
- Weighted criteria.
- Risk factors.
- Total cost model.
- Implementation complexity.
- Operational burden.
- Support burden.
- Vendor maturity.
- Roadmap risk.
- Reversibility.

Closed loop: the organization records how it said it would decide before seeing which answer it prefers.

### 5. The Vendor Claim Register

Every important vendor claim should become a durable row.

Fields:

- Vendor.
- Claim.
- Date/source.
- Who heard or received it.
- Condition or assumption attached to the claim.
- Evidence supplied.
- Verification method.
- Owner responsible for verification.
- Status: unverified, verified, contradicted, accepted risk, not tested.
- Later implementation result.

This is the RFP equivalent of the evidence ledger and risk-find register.

### 6. Proof of Concept Rules

A POC should not be a sales demo with engineering labor attached.

Define:

- What the POC must prove.
- What the POC explicitly does not prove.
- What data and scale are realistic.
- Who runs it.
- Who observes it.
- What counts as failure.
- What counts as inconclusive.
- What assumptions remain after the POC.

Closed loop: the POC produces evidence against pre-stated claims, not vibes.

### 7. Selection Decision Record

The final selection should preserve:

- Why this vendor.
- Why not the others.
- Which tradeoffs were accepted.
- Which risks were accepted.
- Which assumptions must be revisited.
- Which vendor claims are now commitments.
- What the organization expects to be true after implementation.

### 8. Post-Implementation Review

After implementation, compare actuals against:

- Original problem statement.
- RFP requirements.
- Evaluation scorecard.
- Vendor claim register.
- POC findings.
- Implementation plan.
- Cost model.
- Support model.
- Reliability and operability expectations.
- User/operator experience.

The point is not to punish the selection team. The point is to make the next RFP smarter.
