Socratic Leadership Seminar

Create a recurring place where leadership thinking happens.

An implementation guide for creating a recurring place where leadership thinking happens.

Purpose

The Socratic Leadership Seminar is a leadership reading group, not a class, a change initiative, or a disguised planning meeting.

Participants read credible leadership and management research, then meet periodically to explore what the material reveals, where it contradicts their experience, and which questions remain unresolved. The seminar does not attempt to force agreement or produce an action plan. It creates repeated opportunities for leaders to encounter serious ideas, test those ideas against experience, and update their mental models over time.

The fixing, if it happens, is a byproduct.

Where It Fits in Talent Development Architecture

Talent Development Architecture treats growth as a combination of real work, learning from other people, and formal support. For leaders, the seminar is one mechanism for learning from others:

  • 70 percent real work: coaching people and carrying actual management responsibility
  • 20 percent learning from others: peer exploration, mentoring, observation, and the seminar
  • 10 percent formal support: reading, courses, case studies, and other structured study

The ratios are design guidance, not timekeeping requirements.

The seminar cannot replace real management practice or direct coaching. It gives leaders a place to examine the mental models they bring to that work.

Cohorts

Begin with a voluntary group. A useful starting cohort may be current directors and senior leaders, prospective managers, or another leadership population appropriate to the organization.

Current and prospective leaders do not have to be placed in the same cohort. Established-leader seminars can draw heavily on management experience. Leadership-discovery cohorts can help people test whether facilitation, coaching, and the development of others are work they genuinely enjoy.

As participation grows, multiple groups can operate in parallel. Participants should be able to move among compatible groups rather than becoming permanently assigned to one circle.

Source Selection

The material should come from credible sources: peer-reviewed research, university departments, established research organizations, serious management publications, or well-documented organizational case studies.

Source credibility does not require agreement. A polished article may overstate its evidence. A widely used framework may collapse under lived experience. Those tensions are discussion material.

The group should agree on:

  • the meeting cadence
  • a realistic reading load
  • the standard for acceptable sources
  • who selects each session’s material
  • how far in advance the material must be distributed

Two weeks is a useful minimum for distributing the reading. The reading load should be small enough that preparation remains realistic for the full group.

Rotating Facilitation

One participant facilitates each session, with responsibility rotating through the group.

The facilitator selects and distributes the material, opens the discussion, notices connections, surfaces tensions, and protects the purpose of the meeting. Rotation serves two purposes: everyone has to stretch their leadership thinking far enough to choose material worth discussing, and everyone receives practice facilitating a room.

The facilitator is a co-explorer, not a guide leading the group toward a predetermined conclusion.

A useful opening sounds like:

These articles challenged some assumptions I had about change communication. I am curious what everyone else made of them.

The wrong opening is a disguised argument:

  • “I found research that proves my view.”
  • “Let us decide how to apply this.”
  • “Here is what everyone should take away.”

Session Shape

A 60-minute session is a useful default. Extend it only when group size and discussion quality justify the additional time.

Open with genuine curiosity

Spend the first few minutes asking what struck people, what seemed true, what felt overstated, and what created disagreement.

Useful prompts include:

  • What jumped out at you?
  • Did anything surprise you?
  • Where do you disagree?
  • Does this match your experience or contradict it?

Do not organize the answers too early. Let the group’s first reactions reveal where the energy is.

Let the discussion find its energy

The facilitator should:

  1. Notice connections. Bring earlier comments back when two participants appear to be describing related ideas.
  2. Surface tensions. Make competing interpretations visible without rushing to resolve them.
  3. Invite lived experience. Ask where participants have seen the idea play out without turning one person’s experience into a referendum on the whole organization.
  4. Return to the research. Introduce a provocative claim, method, or finding when the discussion loses energy.
  5. Let disagreement breathe. Ask people to explain what they do not accept instead of defending the material.

Real workplace examples are welcome when they deepen the discussion. One person’s current problem should not take over the session or turn the group into an ad hoc working meeting.

Close with an open question

Do not end by asking what everyone will do differently.

Ask instead:

  • What are you still thinking about?
  • What question did we leave unresolved?
  • Which idea became less clear after discussing it?

The seminar succeeds when participants leave with a better question, a productive disagreement, or an idea that returns to them later.

ELMO: Enough, Let’s Move On

It is a sign of success when participants begin imagining practical applications. It is also the point where the seminar can accidentally become a planning meeting.

Any participant may say ELMO: Enough, Let’s Move On when the discussion has moved from exploring the material into designing a solution. ELMO is not used to suppress disagreement or prevent real examples. It protects the seminar from being consumed by implementation work that belongs somewhere else.

If someone wants to try an idea, the facilitator can ask what they imagine trying. The group does not have to design the experiment during the seminar.

The Whiteboard

Use the whiteboard minimally. Its purpose is to hold threads of thought while the conversation unfolds.

Useful entries include:

  • themes participants notice
  • questions that surface
  • tensions or contradictions
  • brief examples from experience
  • a parking lot for important topics that do not fit the session

The board should not drive toward decisions, owners, deadlines, or action items. At the end of the session, it is evidence of thinking, not a plan.

A photograph of the board may be shared with the next session’s topic. No additional artifact is required.

Facilitator Discipline

The hardest facilitation requirement is genuine curiosity. The facilitator must be willing for experienced leaders to reject the material, interpret it differently, or reach no conclusion at all.

The useful stance is not “these people need to learn this.” It is “these people have experience the research may not capture; I want to understand what happens when the two meet.”

Questions that preserve that stance include:

  • What systemic pressure makes the recommended behavior difficult?
  • What does lived experience reveal that the research does not?
  • Why might a reasonable leader choose the apparently weaker approach?
  • Which assumption does the disagreement expose?

Measurement Boundary

The seminar is a deliberately unmeasured input. Attendance proves presence. A worksheet proves that a worksheet was completed. Neither proves that a leader’s mental model changed.

That does not make the seminar unfalsifiable. Its effects, if any, should eventually appear downstream: better coaching behavior, more thoughtful allocation of developmental reps, stronger facilitation, useful references to earlier discussions, or participants voluntarily requesting the next reading.

Do not attribute those outcomes directly to the seminar. The causal claim would be stronger than the evidence. The honest signal is that ideas return without being forced.

The failure mode is not that the seminar lacks a score. The failure mode is measuring everything so aggressively that the organization stops investing in forms of learning that do not measure cleanly.

The Long Game

The value compounds across sessions.

After several meetings, participants may begin connecting new material to earlier conversations. Later, they may bring their own research, test an idea in real work, or recognize a management pattern they previously lacked language for.

The seminar does not exist to fix leaders. It creates a recurring place where leadership thinking happens. That is enough work for one room.
