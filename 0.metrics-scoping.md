# Metrics Scoping — A Sabermetrics-for-Talent Research Brief

Purpose: scope the "system metrics" chapter of TDA. Answers one question honestly — *how much of
sports analytics can you actually port to measuring talent development, and where does it hit a wall?*

Bottom line up front: **you will not find one number. You will build a stack, the way the NFL did.**
And one of the NFL's three layers is genuinely unbuildable for business — knowing that up front saves
you months.

---

## 1. Why football, not baseball (the attribution problem)

Baseball is easy to measure for one structural reason: it's a sequence of near-isolated 1-v-1 events
(pitcher vs. batter). Credit attribution is almost free — the outcome belongs to two people.

Football is the *credit-attribution-under-interdependence* problem: 11 coordinated assignments per
play, one blown assignment detonates the whole play, and the box score (yards, points) tells you the
*result* but not *whose contribution*. This is exactly the business problem: a shipped feature, a
survived incident, a retained customer — the outcome is real but the credit is entangled across people,
dependencies, luck, and "assignment quality" (were they even put in a position to succeed).

This is why NFL analytics lagged baseball by ~20 years and why it never resolved into a single stat.
It resolved into a **stack of three families**, and that stack is your template.

---

## 2. The three layers of NFL analytics → what ports to talent

### Layer 1 — Charting / grading (PFF model). PORTS DIRECTLY. You already have the primitive.
PFF grades every player on every play, by human film review, on a per-assignment basis independent of
outcome (a corner who plays perfect coverage gets a good grade even if the QB makes an impossible
throw). This *decouples process quality from result* — the single most important idea for you, because
it's how you credit a good rep that had a bad outcome.

- **Business analogue:** your **Performance Evidence Ledger IS the charting layer.** The
  observed-behavior / outcome-measure / self-report / interpretation / assumption labels are exactly
  PFF's discipline of grading the assignment, not the box score.
- **What's missing to make it a metric:** charting is only comparable if the *grading rubric is
  shared* (PFF trains graders to a standard). Your Competency Calibration is the rubric. So the metric
  is: ledger entries scored against calibrated competencies, aggregated over time. **Buildable now.**
- **Known weakness (inherit it honestly):** PFF grades are subjective and criticized as such. Yours
  will be too. Mitigation is the same: published rubric, multiple graders on high-stakes calls,
  transparency that it's judgment not physics.

### Layer 2 — Expected-value models (EPA / WPA / success rate). INVENTABLE, this is the hard-but-real work.
EPA (Expected Points Added) asks: given the game state (down, distance, field position), what was the
*expected* points value before this play, and after? The play's value = the change. WPA does the same
with win probability. Success rate is the binary cousin (did this play gain enough to stay "on
schedule").

The key insight: **value is measured as change in expected outcome given the starting state, not as
raw output.** A 4-yard gain on 3rd-and-3 is a success; the same 4 yards on 3rd-and-8 is a near-failure.
Context defines value.

- **Business analogue — "Expected Development Added":** given a person's starting state (current
  operating mode, competency baseline, the difficulty of the rep), did this assignment move their
  expected capability forward more or less than expected? A junior owning a reversible fix is a
  "success" the way 4-yards-on-3rd-and-3 is; the same task given to a staff engineer added ~0.
- **This is exactly your transition-gate model already** (seeing→reporting→fixing→preventing→
  automating). Those gates ARE the "down and distance." You've built the state space; the metric is
  putting a value on advancing through it, weighted by the difficulty of the gate.
- **What it takes to build:** you need a *baseline model of expected progression* — how long/how many
  reps does moving from mode N to N+1 usually take, for people at this stage? That requires data you
  don't have yet. Early version is a hand-set prior (expert judgment: "a helpdesk→junior transition is
  ~2 quarters of safe reps"); mature version is fit from accumulated ledger data. **Buildable
  incrementally — starts as a prior, earns its coefficients over time.** This is the chapter's center.
- **Honest caveat:** EPA works because the NFL has millions of plays to fit the expected-points curve.
  You will have dozens of people. Your model will be *coarse and Bayesian*, not fitted and precise, for
  a long time. State that. It's still better than the box score (retention alone).

### Layer 3 — Opponent adjustment (DVOA). DOES NOT PORT. Declare it unbuildable.
DVOA adjusts every performance for strength of opponent, made possible by a **closed league with a
shared schedule** — every team plays a comparable slate, so you can normalize. This is what lets you
say a stat is good "adjusted for who they faced."

- **Why it dies for business:** there is no league, no common opponent, no shared schedule. Two
  engineers on two teams face non-comparable "opponents" (problem domains, tech debt, dependency drag,
  managers). There is no population-level normalizer because there is no population playing the same
  games. You cannot build relative-value-vs-league the way DVOA does.
- **The move:** *declare this a stated design boundary,* the same way TDA already refuses to pretend
  business is as clean as box scores. Don't chase a league that doesn't exist. Within-org, small
  same-role cohorts (see §4) are the closest honest substitute, and they're weak — treat them as
  directional, not adjudicating.

## 3. What each layer measures — and which of your gaps it closes

| Layer | Sports | Your version | Buildable? | Closes which gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Charting | PFF grades | Evidence Ledger × Calibration rubric | **Now** | System metrics (process quality) |
| Expected value | EPA / WPA | "Expected Development Added" over transition gates | **Incrementally** (prior → fitted) | System metrics (the real one) + adoption-as-behavior |
| Opponent adj. | DVOA | — no league — | **No. Declare boundary.** | (would've been fairness; use §4 instead) |

Note the fairness gap from the revision notes isn't a *layer* — it's a **distribution cut** across the
charting layer: not "how good is the grade" but "who is even getting graded / getting reps." See §4.

---

## 4. The fairness/access metric (folds into the same data)
Once reps are logged (charting layer), the fairness instrument is nearly free and it's *leading*, not
lagging:
- **Rep-allocation distribution:** count safe-reps / stretch assignments per person per period.
  Look for the long tail of people getting zero, and the manager who has developed no one.
- **Farm-system access:** who gets cross-team reps vs. who's legible to only one manager. Uneven
  visibility here is the mechanism that turns discretion into inequity.
This is falsifiable, requires no new instrumentation beyond the ledger, and is the thing satisfaction
surveys can never show you because it's about opportunity *before* outcome.

## 5. Lagging metrics — collect, but demote them
Retention, satisfaction/eNPS, internal mobility rate, "we produce good engineers" showing up in OKRs:
real, worth gathering, but **lagging, low-resolution, and confounded** (retention moves for comp,
managers, the macro economy — not just development). These are the box score. Use them as *guardrails
and tie-breakers*, never as the primary signal, and never let a good box score hide a broken process
(the whole point of the charting layer is that output can lie about process).

---

## 6. Recommended build order (so the chapter has a spine)
1. **Ship the charting layer first** — it's already 80% built (Ledger + Calibration). Make grades
   aggregate-able. This alone beats what most orgs have.
2. **Add rep-allocation distribution** — nearly free, closes fairness, and it's leading.
3. **Hand-set the Expected-Development priors** — expert judgment on typical gate-transition effort.
   Start coarse. This is the "EPA v0."
4. **Let the priors earn coefficients** — as ledger data accumulates, fit the expected-progression
   curve. This is the multi-year research payoff and the genuine sabermetric.
5. **Never build opponent adjustment.** State the boundary. Use small same-role cohorts as directional
   context only.

## 7. The honest wall (say this out loud in the chapter)
The thing you want "the way you want it" — a clean, fitted, opponent-adjusted, single-number talent
sabermetric — **cannot be built for business,** and not because you're not smart enough: because the
structural preconditions (isolated events, millions of samples, a closed league) don't exist. What CAN
be built is a *stack* — subjective-but-calibrated grades + a coarse Bayesian expected-development model
+ a leading fairness distribution — that is honest about being judgment-assisted-by-data rather than
physics. That is exactly what the NFL settled for, and the NFL is the right analogy precisely because
*it also couldn't get to baseball's cleanliness and stopped pretending it could.*
